📋 What Happened — Background

April 08, 2026

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched a massive, coordinated preemptive strike on Iran — codenamed Operation Epic Fury (US) and Operation Roaring Lion (Israel). At approximately 7:00 AM local time, the United States and Israel began conducting extensive strikes against a wide range of targets in Iran. The strikes were dubbed Operation Epic Fury by the United States and Operation Roaring Lion by Israel. The outset was communicated unconventionally: President Trump announced the attacks in a Truth Social post at 2:00 AM EST, with no formal address to the media or public briefing to Congress beyond a notification to the Gang of Eight shortly before strikes commenced.

U.S. and Israeli forces launched nearly 900 strikes in 12 hours targeting Iranian missiles, air defenses, military infrastructure, and leadership. The initial wave of strikes killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and dozens of other officials, but also killed about 170 people when a missile struck a girls' school adjacent to a naval base in Minab, near Bandar Abbas. Strikes also killed the commander of the Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), and the country's defense minister.

Just before the strikes began, Oman's Foreign Minister Badr Al-Busaidi said a "breakthrough" had been reached and Iran had agreed both to never stockpile enriched uranium and to full verification by the IAEA; furthermore, Iran had agreed to irreversibly downgrade its current enriched uranium. Al-Busaidi said peace was "within reach." The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) said that while Iran has an "ambitious" nuclear program and has refused to allow inspections of its damaged sites since the 2025 war, there was no evidence of a structured nuclear weapons program when the 2026 war began.

Iran responded with missile and drone strikes against Israel, US bases, and US-allied countries in the Middle East, and by closing the Strait of Hormuz, disrupting global trade. Mojtaba Khamenei was elected on 8 March 2026 to replace his father as supreme leader, and the IRGC, as well as Iran's top leaders pledged their allegiance to him. The campaign expanded to include Iranian proxy fronts in Lebanon, Iraq, and Yemen.

🕊️ CEASEFIRE — April 7–8, 2026 (Day 39–40): President Trump announced via Truth Social that the United States and Iran had reached an agreement based on a 10-point proposal mediated by Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif and General Asim Munir. The United States, Israel, and Iran agreed to a two-week ceasefire, under which Iran would also re-open the Strait of Hormuz; while the announcement occurred on 7 April in Washington, the acceptance and official implementation took effect on 8 April local time in the Middle East. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu however asserted that the ceasefire does not apply to Lebanon, contradicting Sharif's announcement. The immediate next step is the start of negotiations in Islamabad, where US and Iranian officials are expected to meet under Pakistani mediation.

✅ Arguments For the War

🛡️
Iran's offensive capabilities significantly degraded

An analysis by JINSA showed a sharp reduction in Iranian missile attacks after the first day of the war. The number of Iranian missiles aimed at Israel was far lower than during the Twelve-Day War in 2025, attributed to joint US-Israeli efforts eliminating as many as three-quarters of Iranian missile launchers.

☢️
Nuclear and military infrastructure destroyed

Admiral Brad Cooper confirmed the US military had struck more than 8,000 Iranian military targets, including 130 vessels. On 21 March, the US conducted strikes on the Natanz Nuclear Facility using bunker buster bombs.

🤝
Ceasefire and diplomacy achieved within 40 days

The U.S. and Iran reached a ceasefire deal less than two hours before the deadline President Trump imposed. As part of the agreement, Trump said the U.S. and Israel would suspend bombing Iran for two weeks, subject to Iran reopening the Strait of Hormuz for safe passage. Trump said the US has received a 10-point proposal from Iran, which he called a "workable basis on which to negotiate."

📉
Oil prices fell sharply on ceasefire news

Oil prices plunged by more than 17% after the ceasefire announcement. Costs at the pump had risen sharply for ordinary Americans since the war, putting heavy political pressure on Trump.

🌍
Broad international welcome for cessation of hostilities

UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres called on all parties to abide by the ceasefire terms "in order to pave the way toward a lasting and comprehensive peace in the region," and underscored that "an end to hostilities is urgently needed to protect civilian lives."

❌ Arguments Against the War

⚖️
War launched during active, promising nuclear negotiations

Just before strikes began, Oman's FM said a "breakthrough" had been reached and Iran had agreed to never stockpile enriched uranium and to full IAEA verification. Talks were expected to resume on 2 March. After the attack, Al-Busaidi said "active and serious negotiations" had been undermined.

💀
High civilian toll, cultural and educational sites struck

As of 27 March, Iran reports damage to at least 120 historical sites. An analysis by BBC Verify suggests a deadly attack on residential buildings and a sports hall in Lamerd, killing 21 people including 4 children and injuring 100, was carried out using a Precision Strike Missile (PrSM), a US munition. On April 4, the Laser and Plasma Research Institute of Shahid Beheshti University in Tehran was bombed. The university called it "a clear attack on reason, research, and freedom of thought."

🌊
Strait of Hormuz closure caused unprecedented economic shock

The war's economic impact, described as the world's largest supply disruption since the 1970s energy crisis, includes surges in oil and gas prices, wide disruptions in aviation and tourism, and volatility in financial markets. Ras Laffan, the world's largest LNG export complex, had 17% of Qatar's export capacity knocked offline, with repairs expected to take three to five years according to QatarEnergy's chief executive.

🚫
Ceasefire terms remain deeply disputed and fragile

In the version of Iran's 10-point ceasefire plan released in Persian, Iran included the phrase "acceptance of enrichment" regarding uranium needed for its nuclear programme, but that phrase was missing from English versions shared by Iranian diplomats. Trump initially called the plan "workable" but later called it fraudulent, without elaborating. Israeli officials were skeptical and believed the breakthrough could unravel if Iran does not make good on quickly opening the Strait of Hormuz.

⚠️
Threats to civilian infrastructure raised war crime concerns

Prior to the ceasefire, Trump threatened to destroy bridges, power plants, and water treatment facilities. Wide-scale destruction of such infrastructure, without distinction between civilian and military targets, would be considered a war crime under international and U.S. law, legal experts told NPR.

🇱🇧
Lebanon front remains active — ceasefire scope disputed

Israel renewed its strikes on southern Lebanon on April 8 as Netanyahu insisted the Iran war truce does not include Lebanon. Israeli opposition leader Yair Lapid blasted the ceasefire, saying "there has never been such a political disaster in our entire history," and criticized Netanyahu for failing "to meet a single one of the goals he set for himself."

🌍 How the World Has Responded

🇺🇸 United States (Trump admin.)

Trump declared: "This will be a double sided CEASEFIRE! The reason for doing so is that we have already met and exceeded all Military objectives, and are very far along with a definitive Agreement concerning Longterm PEACE with Iran."

🇮🇱 Israel (Netanyahu)

Netanyahu's office said it supports Trump's decision to suspend strikes against Iran for two weeks, but insisted the ceasefire doesn't include Lebanon. Israeli opposition leader Yair Lapid blasted the deal, saying "there has never been such a political disaster in our entire history" and that Netanyahu "failed to meet a single one of the goals he set for himself."

🇮🇷 Iran (New leadership)

Iranian leaders touted the ceasefire as a victory, noting the US agreed to "the general framework" of Iran's 10-point proposal. The Supreme National Security Council said: "Good news to the dear nation of Iran! Nearly all the objectives of the war have been achieved." Iran's SNSC also emphasized "this does not signify the termination of the war" and that "our hands remain upon the trigger."

🇵🇰 Pakistan (Mediator)

Pakistani PM Shehbaz Sharif announced: "The Islamic Republic of Iran and the United States of America, along with their allies, have agreed to an immediate ceasefire everywhere including Lebanon and elsewhere, EFFECTIVE IMMEDIATELY," and invited both delegations to Islamabad on April 10 to finalize a conclusive agreement.

🇷🇺 Russia

Russia said the approach of a "one-track, aggressive, unprovoked attack" against Iran suffered a "crushing defeat" after Trump announced the ceasefire. "Once again, this position has suffered a crushing defeat," Foreign Ministry spokeswoman Maria Zakharova told Sputnik Radio.

🇨🇳 China

China welcomed the ceasefire, underlining its role in encouraging a deal. "China welcomes relevant parties announcing the reaching of a ceasefire agreement," Foreign Ministry spokeswoman Mao Ning told a news conference.

🇪🇺 European Union

European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen welcomed the ceasefire, saying it "brings much needed de-escalation." EU Council President Antonio Costa urged all parties to uphold its terms for "sustainable peace," while EU foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas called the deal "a step back from the brink after weeks of escalation."

🇬🇧 United Kingdom

UK PM Starmer said he did "not believe in regime change from the skies." The UK has not participated militarily in the strikes; UK aircraft have been deployed only in a defensive role to intercept missiles and projectiles in Qatar, Jordan, Iraq, and Cyprus.

🇯🇵 Japan

Japan's Chief Cabinet Secretary Minoru Kihara welcomed the two-week ceasefire as a "positive move" and said de-escalation of hostilities in the Middle East remains a top priority.

🇪🇸 Spain

Spanish Prime Minister Pedro Sanchez said "the Spanish government will not applaud those who set the world on fire just because they turn up with a bucket."

🇺🇳 United Nations

UN Secretary-General Guterres and several uninvolved countries condemned the US–Israeli strikes; the United Nations Security Council later passed a resolution condemning Iran's retaliatory strikes on the Gulf states.

❓ What Remains Deeply Uncertain

As of April 8, 2026 (Day 40), the two-week Islamabad Accords ceasefire is nominally in effect, but its durability is deeply uncertain. The ceasefire is temporary and details remain sparse. Iran's 10-point proposal and Washington's stated positions retain substantial differences, and the Islamabad talks carry no guarantee of success. In the version of Iran's plan released in Persian, Iran included "acceptance of enrichment" regarding uranium, but that phrase was missing from English versions shared by Iranian diplomats. Trump initially called the plan "workable" but later called it fraudulent, without elaborating.

It is unclear where negotiations stand regarding Iran's position that it can continue its enrichment of uranium. According to Iranian officials, the proposal requires the lifting of all sanctions and UN resolutions against Iran, alongside the release of Iranian assets held overseas. Other demands include the withdrawal of U.S. combat forces from military bases across the region, compensation in the form of reparations, and Iran's right to nuclear enrichment. Key open questions include: whether formal Islamabad talks on April 10 will yield a durable framework; whether Israel will honor the ceasefire in Lebanon; whether Iran's IRGC will comply given its history of independent command decisions; and whether the enrichment gap can be bridged.

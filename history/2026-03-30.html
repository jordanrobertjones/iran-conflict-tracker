📋 What Happened — Background

March 30, 2026

On February 27, Omani foreign minister Badr Al-Busaidi announced a "breakthrough" in talks where Iran agreed to never stockpile enriched uranium and allow full IAEA verification, with peace described as "within reach." However, after the US and Israel attacked Iran, Al-Busaidi said he was dismayed that "active and serious negotiations" had been undermined. The strikes began on February 28, 2026, when Israel and the United States launched attacks on targets across Iran, codenamed Operation Roaring Lion in Israel and Operation Epic Fury in the United States. The current campaign has damaged the Natanz nuclear facility, the one in Fordow remains inoperable, and defense industrial facilities that would be needed to reconstitute enrichment capacity are being systematically targeted. More than 9,000 targets have been struck since the launch of Operation Epic Fury, according to a March 23, 2026, fact sheet from U.S. Central Command, alongside more than 9,000 combat flights.

✅ Arguments For the War

Significant degradation of Iranian military capabilities: Israeli officials assess that roughly 80 to 85 percent of Iran's air-defense architecture — including radars, interceptors, and detection systems — has been destroyed, giving Israeli aircraft broad freedom of action across much of Iranian airspace. Missile production infrastructure has been hit so extensively that Israeli officials assess Iran currently lacks meaningful reconstitution capacity; before the war, Iran was estimated to produce roughly 100 ballistic missiles per month, and that capacity is now zero. U.S. and Israeli airstrikes have neutralized over 70 percent of Iran's missile launchers and driven missile production to zero, and have eliminated most of Iran's defense industrial base, reportedly setting back several key Iranian defense programs by a matter of years.
Reduced immediate nuclear threat: The Natanz nuclear facility has sustained damage, the Fordow facility remains inoperable, and defense industrial facilities needed to reconstitute enrichment capacity are being systematically targeted.
High Israeli public support: A March 2026 survey by the Israel Democracy Institute found that 82% of the Israeli public supported ongoing military operations, including 93% of Jewish Israelis.
Regional allies now view Iran as direct threat: Most countries in the region, including Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, Qatar, Kuwait and Bahrain, now view the Iranian regime as a direct and a long-term threat to their safety.

❌ Arguments Against the War

Massive humanitarian and civilian cost: At least 1,937 people have been killed by US-Israeli attacks on Iran since February 28, according to Iran's Health Ministry. The deadliest single incident occurred in the city of Minab in southeastern Iran, where a strike on an elementary girls' school killed more than 170 people, most of them schoolgirls. More than 1,750 people have been killed in Iran since the war began, with at least 1,455 civilians, including 217 children, and 1,167 military personnel killed.
War unlikely to achieve stated regime change goal: A solid 51 percent of academic experts expected the Iranian regime to survive in something like its prewar form—an outcome that is looking increasingly likely and that scholars anticipated far better than the pundits or Israeli and U.S. officials. German Chancellor Friedrich Merz said the war is unlikely to lead to "regime change" in Iran, noting "If that's the goal, I don't think you'll achieve it. It's mostly gone wrong" in past conflicts.
Severe global economic disruption: The closure of commercial traffic through the Strait of Hormuz caused oil prices to surge, with Brent crude rising from $73 on February 27 to $107 on March 8 – more than 40 percent increase in 10 days. Approximately 2,000 vessels and 20,000 seafarers are stranded in the Strait of Hormuz, creating severe logistical and humanitarian challenges.
Regional escalation beyond original scope: The conflict has escalated across the Middle East, with Yemen's Houthis joining the war by firing missiles at Israel on Saturday. The violence in Lebanon displaced hundreds of thousands of people and killed hundreds, with the number of displaced people in Lebanon topping 800,000 as Israel issued forced evacuation orders.
Iran continues to demonstrate resilience: One month into the war, Iran has proved that its power was underestimated by the enemy, who thought that the war would be a short excursion and that the country would capitulate after just a few days of bombardment. Given that the Islamic Republic is more likely to continue fighting than to concede, the war's trajectory may prove more protracted and unpredictable than decision-makers in Washington anticipated.
Concerns over escalation and uncontrolled conflict: If the strait stays closed for a considerable period of time, then prices will stay elevated, global production and trade will slow, and the world might enter a recession.

❓ What Remains Deeply Uncertain

What is less clear is how well the Israeli goal of regime change matches the United States' objectives, or if it does, how long that will remain the case. Academic experts expected the Iranian regime to survive in something like its prewar form—an outcome that is looking increasingly likely and that scholars anticipated far better than the pundits or Israeli and U.S. officials. The fighting might have dire effects on the global economy, or it might not; it is too soon to tell. The war continues even as efforts to reach a diplomatic solution increase and there are conflicting claims about possible negotiations, with the US signaling both military escalation and openness to a deal.

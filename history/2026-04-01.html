📋 What Happened — Background

April 01, 2026

On February 27, 2026, President Trump ordered Operation Epic Fury from Air Force One. Trump gave the order at 3:38 p.m. EST, and U.S. missiles, drones, and Israeli fighter jets began striking Iran the next day around 9:45 a.m. IRST. Israel codenamed its parallel campaign Operation Roaring Lion. The operation marked the most significant U.S. military action in the Middle East since the Iraq War, executed without a formal address to Congress beyond a War Powers notification and a Gang of Eight briefing.

Just before strikes began, Oman's Foreign Minister Badr Al-Busaidi declared a diplomatic "breakthrough" had been reached and Iran had agreed to never stockpile enriched uranium and to full IAEA verification; he said peace was "within reach" and talks were expected to resume on March 2. After the attack commenced, Al-Busaidi said he was dismayed and that "active and serious negotiations" had been undermined.

On February 28, Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was assassinated in an Israeli air attack on his compound; Iranian state media confirmed his death on March 1. His daughter, son-in-law, grandchild, and daughter-in-law were also reported killed. Mojtaba Khamenei was elected on March 8, 2026 to replace his father as supreme leader, and Iran's top leaders pledged allegiance to him.

The opening phase focused on decapitating Iranian leadership and degrading missile infrastructure. In the hours that followed, Iran launched large-scale retaliation, expanding the conflict beyond Iranian territory into a region-wide exchange touching multiple Gulf states and allied military assets. The conflict has rapidly widened into a sustained regional war.

By mid-March, Admiral Brad Cooper stated the U.S. military had struck more than 8,000 Iranian military targets, including 130 vessels. On March 19, the cost of the war to the U.S. military was estimated at $18 billion, and the Pentagon requested an additional $200 billion for the war. Iran's Health Ministry has reported at least 1,937 people killed by U.S.-Israeli attacks since February 28.

The Strait of Hormuz has been disrupted since the strikes began, with Iran's IRGC prohibiting vessel passage and launching retaliatory strikes on Gulf states. The 2026 Iran war, including the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, has been characterized by the IEA as the "largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market."

As of April 1, 2026 (Day 33): The U.S. and Iran are discussing a potential deal that would involve a ceasefire in exchange for Iran reopening the Strait of Hormuz, three U.S. officials told Axios. Tehran denied requesting a ceasefire; Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi told Al Jazeera that Iran is not looking for a ceasefire but is seeking to end the war. A third U.S. aircraft carrier strike group departed Virginia for the Middle East Tuesday. International human rights experts and U.N. officials have warned that Trump's threats to strike power plants constitute an open threat to possibly commit a war crime. The April 6 deadline for Iran to reopen the strait — or face destruction of its energy plants — is now five days away.

✅ Arguments For the War

🛡️
Iran's nuclear program severely degraded: Operation Midnight Hammer in June 2025 had already dealt a significant blow, decimating Iran's enrichment facilities at Fordow and Natanz and destroying metallurgy facilities at Isfahan. On March 21, 2026, the U.S. conducted further strikes on the Natanz Nuclear Facility using bunker buster bombs. Israel's Atomic Energy Commission assessed that U.S. strikes on Fordow rendered the enrichment facility inoperable, and that combined strikes have "set back Iran's ability to develop nuclear weapons by many years."
⚔️
Rapid degradation of Iranian military capability: By the tenth day of the war, Iranian missile and drone attacks had dropped by more than 90%, a reduction steeper than during the 2025 conflict, credited to effective suppression by U.S. and Israeli missions. U.S. forces confirmed Iran's naval capability in the Gulf of Oman has been neutralized, with all 11 previously active warships reportedly sunk.
🤝
Allied Gulf states rallying and broad coalition forming: A statement on March 19 from the UK, France, Germany and other nations cited "readiness to contribute to appropriate efforts to ensure safe passage through the Strait of Hormuz," and at least 35 countries are now participating in those efforts. The E3 (UK, France, Germany) resolved to back "proportionate military defensive measures" against Iranian drones and ballistic missiles, signaling potential direct involvement.
🗳️
Strong domestic Israeli public support: A March 2026 survey by the Israel Democracy Institute found that 82% of the Israeli public supported ongoing military operations, including 93% of Jewish Israelis.
📉
Iran's leadership and decision-making severely disrupted: In late March, the New York Times described Iran's leadership as paralyzed with a severely disrupted decision-making process, and reported that damage to communications infrastructure caused paranoia and internal power struggles.

❌ Arguments Against the War

💀
Significant civilian casualties and humanitarian crisis: Iran's Health Ministry reports at least 1,937 people killed by U.S.-Israeli attacks. By March 5, the WHO had identified 13 Iranian health infrastructure sites hit during the war. As of March 27, Iran reported damage to at least 120 historical sites by U.S.-Israeli strikes. A missile strike hit a girls' school in Minab, with reports of significant civilian casualties.
🛢️
Catastrophic global economic damage — worst energy crisis since the 1970s: IEA head Fatih Birol stated that the war has created a deeper glut than the 1970s crises — noting that while those crises each cost about 5 million barrels per day, "today, we lost 12 million barrels per day — more than two of these oil crises put together." Brent crude surged approximately 57% in March 2026, reaching highs of $119.50 per barrel. The conflict has echoed the 1970s energy crisis through acute supply shortages, currency volatility, inflation and heightened risks of stagflation and recession.
☢️
War may be accelerating Iran's nuclear ambitions — NPT withdrawal threat: The intensifying conflict has entered a new and far more dangerous phase; recent airstrikes on Iranian nuclear facilities have triggered a powerful political reaction inside Tehran, with lawmakers now openly pushing for withdrawal from the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty. NPT withdrawal would facilitate reconstitution of Iran's bombed nuclear capabilities and enable Tehran to use them to develop nuclear weapons without international oversight. The IAEA stated there was no evidence of a structured nuclear weapons program when the 2026 war began.
⚖️
International law violations and diplomatic isolation: Israel's legal rationale — that the strikes are a continuation of an existing armed conflict — is contested; international lawyers note it does not account for the ceasefire period in 2025. Norway's Foreign Minister stated the attack breached standards of international law: "Preventive attacks require an immediately imminent threat." The New York Times cited three unnamed U.S. officials with access to intelligence who said Trump exaggerated the immediacy of the Iranian threat; CNN and Reuters had similar reporting.
🌍
Regional escalation and proxy war spreading: Iran has launched strikes across nine countries in the region: Bahrain, Iraq, Jordan, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Israel. On March 28, Yemen's Houthi rebels attacked Israel with a barrage of ballistic missiles — their first such strikes since the war began. The Israeli army confirmed ten soldiers killed in southern Lebanon, where Israeli forces have launched ground operations against Hezbollah.
🗳️
War launched without Congressional authorization and over intelligence dissent: Trump did not seek congressional approval, consistent with his unilateral approach to the June 2025 Iran strikes. Multiple Trump advisors including Steve Bannon and DNI Tulsi Gabbard warned against war with Iran, claiming the intelligence community does not consider Iran to be building a nuclear weapon.

🌍 How the World Has Responded

🇺🇸
United States

Trump announced "major combat operations" aimed at destroying Iran's missiles, navy, and nuclear program. Administration officials offered diverse and changing explanations for the war, including pre-empting Iranian retaliation, preventing nuclear weapons acquisition, and achieving regime change.

🇮🇱
Israel

Netanyahu said the operation "will continue as long as necessary." Defense Minister Katz warned Iran "will pay heavy, increasing prices," and said "attacks in Iran will escalate and expand to additional targets."

🇨🇦
Canada

Prime Minister Mark Carney said Canada supports the U.S. "acting to prevent Iran from obtaining a nuclear weapon and to prevent its regime from further threatening international peace and security."

🇬🇧
United Kingdom

PM Starmer issued a joint statement condemning Iranian counter-strikes and calling for diplomacy, while saying he does "not believe in regime change from the skies." The UK has not participated militarily in offensive strikes. Starmer allowed the U.S. to use British bases for "defensive" strikes.

🇫🇷
France

Macron issued a joint statement condemning Iranian attacks on regional countries but on March 3 said France "cannot approve of" strikes "conducted outside of international law." France deployed Rafale jets to protect military bases in the UAE.

🇩🇪
Germany

The E3 leaders (UK, France, Germany) jointly emphasized they had long urged Iran to end its nuclear program and destabilizing actions. Germany's Defense Minister Boris Pistorius called Trump's labeling of NATO allies as "cowards" unfair, but said Germany could help secure the strait once a ceasefire is agreed.

🇨🇳
China

China's Foreign Minister argued the actions by the U.S. and Israel "violate international law and the basic norms of international relations," calling for an immediate cessation of military action. China and Pakistan jointly presented a peace initiative on April 1.

🇷🇺
Russia

Putin called the targeted killing of Khamenei a "cynical murder." Russia condemned U.S. strikes on Natanz as a "blatant violation of international law."

🇪🇸
Spain

PM Pedro Sánchez rejected "the unilateral military action by the United States and Israel," calling it an escalation contributing to a "more uncertain and hostile international order," and demanded "immediate de-escalation and full respect for international law."

🇳🇴
Norway

Foreign Minister Espen Barth Eide stated the initial attack on Iran by Israel breached international law: "Preventive attacks require an immediately imminent threat."

🇺🇳
United Nations

UN Secretary-General Guterres stated: "The use of force by the United States and Israel against Iran, and the subsequent retaliation by Iran across the region, undermine international peace and security," warning of risks of a wider conflict. The IAEA urged military restraint to "avoid any risk of a nuclear accident" after projectiles landed near the Bushehr Nuclear Power Plant.

🇵🇰
Pakistan

Washington used Pakistan as an intermediary, delivering to Iran a 15-point "action list" — a framework for a possible peace deal. China and Pakistan jointly presented a peace initiative on April 1 to end the war.

❓ What Remains Deeply Uncertain

Will Iran withdraw from the NPT? Iranian lawmakers are openly pushing for NPT withdrawal, with analysts warning the conflict has entered a new and far more dangerous phase for the global nuclear order. Exiting the treaty would eliminate the international legal requirement for Iran to maintain IAEA safeguards, making it exceedingly more difficult to detect future weapons development.

Will the April 6 deadline trigger energy plant strikes? Trump has said if Iran doesn't reopen the strait to all traffic by April 6, he will order the destruction of Iran's energy plants. International human rights experts and U.N. officials say the threat to strike power plants is an open warning of a possible war crime. Iran has threatened to retaliate by striking Gulf desalination plants in return.

Can a ceasefire be reached? Three U.S. officials confirmed the U.S. and Iran are discussing a ceasefire-for-Hormuz deal, though officials cautioned it was unclear whether a deal could be reached. Iran's Foreign Minister said the future of the strait will be decided by Iran and Oman, and that Hormuz won't be opened based on the "absurd displays of the American president."

Has Iran's nuclear program truly been eliminated? Arms Control Association analysts argue that while strikes can destroy infrastructure, military force cannot eliminate Iran's proliferation risk — at the end of the conflict, Iran will retain nuclear expertise and likely key materials necessary for building a bomb. IAEA Director General Grossi told NPR that despite enormous degradation of physical facilities, "most probably, at the end of this [military conflict], the material will still be there and the enrichment capacities will be there."

Economic escalation risk: The emerging view from oil industry executives is that economic fallout could escalate sharply if the Strait of Hormuz isn't reopened within one to three weeks, with enough damage already done to leave prices higher for longer. U.S. government officials and Wall Street analysts have begun considering the prospect that oil prices might surge to an unprecedented $200 a barrel if the conflict is prolonged.

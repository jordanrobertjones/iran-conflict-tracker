📋 What Happened — Background

April 10, 2026

Operations Epic Fury & Roaring Lion began on February 28, 2026, when the United States and Israel launched a massive coordinated air campaign against Iran — codenamed Operation Epic Fury by the U.S. and Operation Roaring Lion by Israel. The operation marked the most significant U.S. military action in the Middle East since the Iraq War, executed without a formal address to Congress beyond a War Powers notification and a Gang of Eight briefing. Just before the strikes began, Oman's Foreign Minister Badr Al-Busaidi said a "breakthrough" had been reached and Iran had agreed to full IAEA verification and to irreversibly downgrade its enriched uranium; Al-Busaidi said peace was "within reach." After the US and Israel attacked Iran, Al-Busaidi said he was dismayed and that "active and serious negotiations" had been undermined.

The stated objectives were to destroy Iran's ballistic missile infrastructure and nuclear program, eliminate IRGC leadership, annihilate Iran's naval capabilities, and neutralize Iranian-backed regional proxies. Strikes were reported on Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei's compound, with his death subsequently confirmed. The opening phase focused on decapitating senior Iranian leadership while degrading missile infrastructure, launch systems, and air defenses. The Israeli Air Force said it struck 500 military targets in western and central Iran, including air defenses and missile launchers, using about 200 fighter jets — the largest combat sortie in its history.

Iran retaliated with hundreds of drones and ballistic missiles at Israel and US military bases in Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates, and struck military bases, oil and gas sites, civilian airports, hotels and ports in a retaliatory campaign that effectively shut the vital Strait of Hormuz waterway. The war and effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz caused the biggest oil supply shock on record, choking off roughly 12 to 15 million barrels of crude oil a day. Thirteen U.S. service members have died during Operation Epic Fury. The conflict between Hezbollah and Israel escalated into the 2026 Lebanon war, killing more than 1,700 civilians and militants by early April.

🕊️ Ceasefire & Islamabad Talks (April 8–10, 2026): On April 7 (U.S. time), President Trump announced a two-week ceasefire based on Iran's 10-point proposal mediated by Pakistani PM Shehbaz Sharif and General Asim Munir. The US, Israel, and Iran agreed to the truce, under which Iran would also re-open the Strait of Hormuz, with implementation taking effect on April 8 local time in the Middle East. Vice President JD Vance will head the U.S. negotiating team for peace talks in Islamabad on Saturday — the highest-level meeting between the US and Iran since the 1979 Islamic revolution. Iranian Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi are expected to lead the Iranian delegation. The ceasefire is already under severe strain: Israel launched its "largest attack across Lebanon" since Operation Roaring Lion began, completing strikes on approximately 100 Hezbollah command centers and military infrastructures across Beirut, the Bekaa, and southern Lebanon. Iran's IRGC said shipping through the Strait of Hormuz stopped following Israeli strikes in Lebanon, which it considered a ceasefire violation. Iran's new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei said Iran will bring management of the Strait of Hormuz "into a new phase" and that Tehran remains determined to "take revenge" for his slain father.

✅ Arguments For the War

🛡️
Iran's Nuclear & Missile Capabilities Severely Degraded

The Israeli military said 70% of Iran's missile launchers were disabled by the 16th day of the war. The Pentagon says it has degraded about 90% of Iran's ballistic missile and drone capabilities. Netanyahu asserted that US–Israeli strikes have eliminated Iran's ability to produce nuclear weapons and ballistic missiles, removing "two existential threats" to Israel.

⚓
Ceasefire Opens Path to Historic Diplomacy

The ceasefire talks in Islamabad represent the highest-level meeting between the US and Iran since the 1979 revolution. White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt said the US has "achieved and exceeded" its core military objectives against Iran during Operation Epic Fury. The White House described the ceasefire as a calculated move, arguing it "created maximum leverage" for Trump to pursue tough negotiations, opening the door to a diplomatic solution and long-term peace.

🌐
Iran's Regional Proxy Network Weakened

Israel carried out decapitation strikes on top Iranian officials and played a central role in suppressing the regime's air defense systems. Admiral Brad Cooper said that the US military struck more than 8,000 Iranian military targets, including 130 vessels. Iran's ability to sustain high-intensity proxy operations has been significantly curtailed.

💥
Iran's Navy Effectively Neutralized

US forces confirmed Iran's naval capability in the Gulf of Oman has been neutralized, with all 11 previously active warships reported sunk. This eliminates a key tool Iran had used to threaten Gulf shipping and project regional power.

❌ Arguments Against the War

⚠️
Ceasefire Immediately Violated — Talks in Peril

The US, Israel and Iran could not agree on the terms on which the ceasefire was forged. Washington and Tehran offered conflicting accounts of whether the Strait of Hormuz was open or closed, and Gulf states reported one of the most intense Iranian attacks of the war. CSIS assessed that the ceasefire is less a resolution than a pause, with the nuclear issue unresolved, Lebanon destabilized, the risk of terrorism persisting, and U.S. alliances strained.

🌊
Strait of Hormuz Control — Potential Strategic Loss for the U.S.

Analysts Niall Ferguson, Richard Haass, and Philip Zelikow wrote that if Iran emerges with any control of the Strait, it would be a "major strategic victory for Iran, more than offsetting the severe damage that has been inflicted on its armed forces since February 28." Former Navy Rear Admiral Mark Montgomery warned that Iran's claim of the right to toll shipping through the Strait violates international law, adding that Trump "doesn't care about that."

☢️
Nuclear Issue Unresolved; Fissile Material Intact

While Trump and Hegseth say the 38-day bombardment achieved military objectives, Iran's fissile material is still in the country, Iran has not agreed to give up its nuclear ambitions, and Iran has not agreed to stop supporting militant proxies in the region. The IAEA said that while Iran has an "ambitious" nuclear program, there was no evidence of an ongoing nuclear weapons program — undermining a key stated rationale for the war.

🏚️
Massive Civilian Casualties, Infrastructure Destruction

Iran's Health Ministry reports at least 2,076 people have been killed by US-Israeli attacks on Iran since February 28, with over 26,500 injured, including at least 4,000 women and 1,621 children. As of March 27, Iran reported damage to at least 120 historical sites by US-Israeli strikes. Iran's Bushehr nuclear power plant had been struck four times, with Foreign Minister Araghchi criticizing a "lack of concern" for nuclear safety.

💸
Catastrophic Global Economic Fallout

The cost of the first 100 hours of Operation Epic Fury alone is estimated at $3.7 billion, or approximately $891.4 million per day. An Iranian missile strike on Qatar's natural gas facilities on March 18 knocked out 17% of the country's LNG export capacity at an estimated cost of $20 billion in lost revenue. QatarEnergy said Iranian strikes on a large gas field will take three to five years to repair.

🤝
U.S. Alliance Relationships Severely Strained

Trump is "clearly disappointed" with many NATO allies for not supporting the war on Iran to the extent he wanted, NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte said. A diplomat said it was useful to have Rutte explain to Trump that NATO allies were "totally surprised" by the war — with some having to evacuate their citizens from the region. Chatham House assessed that the US action has not provided the display of strength the Trump administration hoped for, and has depleted US ammunition stockpiles while showing the limits of existing US anti-drone defense capabilities.

EU foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas said the ceasefire should include Lebanon and that Hezbollah must disarm, stating "Israeli actions are putting the US-Iran ceasefire under severe strain" and that Israeli strikes "killed hundreds last night, making it hard to argue that such heavy-handed actions fall within self-defence."

🇫🇷
FranceCritical

President Macron said he remained concerned about the "critical" situation in Lebanon after Israel continued strikes following the ceasefire, stating it "cannot be a long-term solution." France deployed Rafale jets to protect military bases in the UAE.

🇪🇸

❓ What Remains Deeply Uncertain

As of April 10, 2026 — Day 42 — the following remain deeply uncertain:

  • Will the Islamabad talks succeed? Rhetoric and published demands from both the US and Iran indicate the parties will enter talks demanding near-total capitulation from one another; analysts say it would be a major achievement if VP Vance emerges with anything more than clarified ceasefire terms, and there is a substantial risk talks will lay bare splits that could tear the process apart.
  • Is the Strait of Hormuz actually open? Iran said passage will be "possible via coordination with Iran's Armed Forces." Shipping company executives and analysts told CNN uncertainty is making transit too risky, and Iranian authorities haven't laid out a plan for safe passage.
  • Will Iran's nuclear program reconstitute? Iran's nuclear program has been at the center of the conflict for decades. The US and Israel destroyed much of the program in 2025 and 2026, with much of Iran's facilities buried under rubble — yet Iran has ferociously defended its right to enrich uranium.
  • Will Lebanon bring down the ceasefire? Trita Parsi of the Quincy Institute says Lebanon "is going to be very difficult" — Iran cannot agree to a ceasefire for itself while "leaving Lebanon exposed," making it a "major test" for the US in coming weeks.
  • What is the true state of Iran's regime? Princeton scholar Bernard Haykel outlined three possible scenarios: the regime "survives and hardens," survives in a more moderate form with new leadership willing to cut deals, or falls entirely — which he considers least likely.
  • Risk of expanded terrorism: CSIS warns that US-Israeli strikes have heightened the near-term risk of terrorism by Tehran and its partners, including Hezbollah. Even in a ceasefire environment, the incentive for retaliation is strong, as Tehran has a history of plotting attacks to avenge senior figures, and the recent losses — over 250 high-ranking officials — are unprecedented in scale.

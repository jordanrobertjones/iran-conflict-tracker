📋 What Happened — Background

February 28, 2026

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched coordinated airstrikes against Iran in a joint operation codenamed Operation Epic Fury (U.S.) and Operation Roaring Lion (Israel). The strikes targeted nuclear facilities, ballistic missile infrastructure, radar installations, and military leadership. Iranian Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed in the attacks.

Iran responded with missile and drone counterstrikes targeting Israel, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, the UAE, and Iraq. Air traffic across the region ground to a halt, and Strait of Hormuz shipping flows began to slow.

Key context leading up to the strikes:

The June 2025 "12-Day War" — in which Israel struck Iranian nuclear sites and Iran retaliated — had already established direct military conflict between the two countries. Following that war, Iran experienced massive anti-government protests starting in late December 2025 amid economic collapse. US–Iran nuclear negotiations mediated by Oman collapsed just two days before the February strikes.

✅ Arguments For the War

1

Nuclear Threat Neutralized (For Now)

Supporters argue that Iran was actively reviving its nuclear weapons program. Strikes on Natanz, Fordow, and Esfahan are seen as having set back Iran's ability to develop a nuclear bomb — removing a major regional and global security threat.

2

Weakening of Iranian Military Power

Iran's ballistic missile infrastructure and air defense systems were significantly degraded. Combined with losses in the June 2025 war, Iran's capacity to threaten Israel and U.S. bases in the region has been reduced.

3

Iran's "Axis of Resistance" Already Weakened

Hezbollah and Hamas were decimated in 2024–25. Syria's Assad regime had already fallen. Proponents argue that this was a strategic window to act while Iran was at its most isolated, with fewer proxy forces to retaliate through.

4

Potential for Regime Change & Iranian Freedom

With massive protests already destabilizing the Iranian government before the strikes, some argue the military action could accelerate regime collapse — potentially leading to a freer Iran that no longer sponsors terrorism or pursues nuclear weapons.

5

Self-Defense Framing

Israel characterized the strikes as "preemptive" — acting before Iran could reconstitute its nuclear program. Under international law, anticipatory self-defense is a contested but recognized doctrine when a threat is imminent and credible.

❌ Arguments Against the War

1

Diplomacy Was Abandoned at a Critical Moment

US–Iran nuclear negotiations in Geneva had just concluded — without a deal, but with talks ongoing. The UN Secretary-General said the strikes "squandered an opportunity for diplomacy." Critics argue the U.S. attacked while still at the negotiating table.

2

Risk of Wider Regional War

Iran's retaliatory strikes hit six countries — Israel, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, the UAE, and Iraq. Analysts warn this conflict is "existential" for the Iranian regime and unlikely to end quickly, raising the risk of a broader, prolonged war drawing in multiple nations.

3

Bombing Rarely Produces Regime Change

Experts note that "the decapitation theory has almost no historical support." Populations tend to rally behind their governments under external attack — even when they oppose those governments. The parallel to the 2003 Iraq War is widely cited as a cautionary example.

4

Questions of International Law

Norway's Foreign Minister stated the strikes "are not in line with international law." Russia called them an "unprovoked act of armed aggression against a sovereign state." The UN Security Council convened in emergency session. Iran had not attacked the U.S. directly before the strikes.

5

Nuclear Proliferation Risk

EU analysts warned of "a dangerous nuclear proliferation cascade." Other countries observing these events may conclude that the only way to deter U.S. or Israeli military action is to develop nuclear weapons as quickly as possible — potentially making the world less safe long-term.

6

Damage to U.S. Diplomatic Credibility

Critics argue that attacking a country while in active negotiations signals to future adversaries that U.S. diplomacy cannot be trusted. This may make it harder to negotiate with North Korea, China, or other rivals in the future.

Critical

❓ What Remains Deeply Uncertain

Because this conflict is so recent, many critical questions remain unanswered:

Who leads Iran now? With Khamenei killed, Iran's political succession is unclear. The identity and ideology of a new leadership will shape everything that follows.

Will the protests turn into revolution? Iran was already experiencing its largest protests since 1979. It is unknown whether the strikes will rally Iranians around the remaining government or accelerate its collapse.

How far will Iran retaliate? Iran struck six countries in its initial counterattack. Whether this escalates further — potentially drawing in more actors — remains to be seen.

What happens to Iran's nuclear program? Even if facilities were destroyed, nuclear knowledge and personnel cannot be bombed away. How Iran rebuilds — or pursues a covert path — is unknown.

Arguments For and Against — presented without political bias

📋 What Happened — Background

April 11, 2026

On February 28, 2026, the United States (Operation Epic Fury) and Israel (Operation Roaring Lion) launched a massive coordinated surprise military campaign against Iran during active nuclear negotiations, targeting leadership, military, naval, and nuclear infrastructure. Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed on the first day, along with dozens of senior Iranian officials. Iran retaliated with hundreds of drones and ballistic missiles against Israel, US bases across the Gulf, and regional civilian infrastructure, and closed the Strait of Hormuz — disrupting roughly 20% of global oil supply. The conflict spread across more than a dozen countries, killing over 2,000 people in Iran, more than 1,700 in Lebanon, and 13 US service members. After 40 days of sustained combat, a Pakistan-brokered two-week ceasefire took effect on April 8, 2026. As of April 11, 2026, US and Iranian delegations have arrived in Islamabad for the highest-level direct negotiations between the two nations since 1979. Iran's 71-person delegation is led by Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi and Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf; the US delegation is led by Vice President JD Vance alongside envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner. Iran has set preconditions — including a halt to Israeli strikes in Lebanon and release of frozen Iranian assets — before substantive talks begin. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, with Iran halting shipping in protest of continued Israeli attacks on Lebanon, which it considers a ceasefire violation. The ceasefire is under severe strain.

✅ Arguments For the War

🎯
Nuclear Program Significantly Set Back: US and Israeli strikes decimated enrichment facilities at Fordow, Natanz, and Isfahan (first struck in June 2025), and further damaged remaining nuclear infrastructure. Iran's nuclear weapons timeline has been materially extended, though highly enriched uranium stockpiles buried underground remain unresolved.
⚓
Iranian Naval Capability Destroyed: US forces confirmed Iran's naval capability in the Gulf of Oman was neutralized, including the reported sinking of all 11 active Iranian warships, eliminating a long-standing maritime threat.
🚀
Ballistic Missile Infrastructure Degraded: Israeli and US strikes hit hundreds of missile launcher sites, production facilities, and launch pads. The IDF reported strikes on the South Pars gas field amounting to "tens of billions of dollars" in economic damage to the regime's military-industrial base.
🕊️
Ceasefire Opens Diplomatic Window: The Pakistan-brokered two-week ceasefire (April 8) and the Islamabad talks (April 11) represent the highest-level US-Iran engagement since 1979 and a potential off-ramp. Trump's 15-point proposal and Iran's 10-point counter-plan form competing frameworks for a lasting deal.
🛡️
Proxy Network Weakened: The war continued a multi-year campaign degrading Iran's "Axis of Resistance." Hezbollah has lost most of its senior leadership, and Shia militia groups in Iraq lack capacity for coherent operations without direct Iranian state support.
🌍
Gulf States Unified on Security: Gulf oil-producing nations and European states have coalesced around two key demands: an end to Israeli attacks on Lebanon and opposition to Iranian tolls on Hormuz shipping, potentially strengthening a new regional security architecture.

❌ Arguments Against the War

⚖️
Diplomacy Was Actively Undermined: On February 27, just hours before strikes began, Oman's Foreign Minister confirmed Iran had agreed to zero uranium stockpiling and full IAEA verification — a major breakthrough. Arms Control Association analysts concluded it is "unlikely that any outcome short of complete Iranian capitulation" would have averted the strikes, calling US negotiators technically unprepared.
💀
Mass Civilian Casualties and War Crimes Allegations: Iran reports over 2,000 killed and 26,500 wounded. A US strike hit an elementary school in Minab on day one, killing at least 168 children and 14 teachers — with NYT reporting preliminary evidence the strike used outdated targeting data. Universities, hospitals, and cultural sites were also struck. Amnesty International declared Iran's use of child soldiers a war crime; Iran called the school strike a war crime.
🛢️
Largest Oil Supply Shock on Record: Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz choked off 12–15 million barrels/day. Global oil prices surged. The Philippines declared a national energy emergency. Japan released historic emergency petroleum reserves. US Asian allies are now buying Russian oil and negotiating directly with Iran and China — reshuffling long-term energy partnerships away from the US.
🌐
Regime Survived; Strategic Goals Unmet: Despite killing Khamenei and top IRGC leadership, the Iranian political system did not collapse. Mojtaba Khamenei was rapidly installed as new Supreme Leader. The population did not mobilize against the regime as projected. CSIS and Al Jazeera analysts assessed the campaign "fell short" of its declared objectives, with Iran's security apparatus proving "more durable than anticipated."
💸
Enormous Financial Cost: The war cost the US military an estimated $18 billion by March 19, with the Pentagon requesting an additional $200 billion. Arab countries bore over $120 billion in costs by March 31. Iran's HEU stockpile is now assessed as a greater proliferation risk than before the war, as the pre-war Geneva deal would have removed it.
🔥
Regional Conflagration and Ceasefire Fragility: The conflict spread to Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Iraq, Jordan, Cyprus (UK base), and Lebanon. Over 1,700 were killed in Lebanon alone. The April 8 ceasefire is under severe strain: Israel continues bombing Lebanon (254 killed on April 9 in a single day), the Strait remains blocked, and Iran accuses the US and Israel of violations. The Islamabad talks face significant preconditions from Iran before substantive negotiations begin.
🏛️
No Congressional Authorization; International Law Disputed: The war was launched without a formal Congressional declaration, with only a War Powers notification and a Gang of Eight briefing. Germany's President Steinmeier called it a "violation of international law." China and Russia condemned it at the UN Security Council. Neither the US nor Israel provided a UN Security Council mandate or invoked Article 51 self-defense with credible legal basis, according to legal analysts.

🌍 How the World Has Responded

🇮🇱
IsraelSupporting

PM Netanyahu confirmed Israel's role as a joint partner ("Roaring Lion"), thanked Trump for "historic leadership," and confirmed support for the ceasefire — but explicitly excluded Lebanon, continuing bombardment. Ceasefire is Netanyahu's political boost: his approval rating rose to 62%.

🇸🇦
Saudi ArabiaSupporting (with conditions)

Crown Prince MBS reportedly lobbied Trump repeatedly for the attack in phone calls. Saudi Arabia was thanked by Australian leaders for facilitating the ceasefire. Riyadh called for a lasting solution preventing Iran from acquiring nuclear weapons or destabilizing the region. Saudi air defenses intercepted dozens of Iranian drones and missiles.

🇵🇰
PakistanMediator

PM Shehbaz Sharif brokered the April 8 ceasefire through weeks of behind-the-scenes diplomacy and is hosting the April 11 Islamabad talks — described as the highest-level US-Iran engagement since 1979. Pakistan Air Force provided escort for the Iranian delegation's flight to Islamabad.

🇦🇪
UAE / Gulf StatesMixed

UAE suffered Iranian drone and missile strikes, including an attack on its US consulate and AWS data center. Gulf states and European nations are uniting on two demands: end Israel's Lebanon attacks and oppose Iranian transit tolls on Hormuz. Kuwait's foreign ministry called on Iran and its proxies to cease all hostilities.

🇨🇳
ChinaOpposed

Foreign Minister Wang Yi made 26 calls with parties and sent a special Middle East envoy. China co-issued a 5-point peace initiative with Pakistan. Beijing condemned the strikes, called Khamenei's killing "a grave violation of Iran's sovereignty," and called the root cause "the U.S.-Israeli launch of military attacks against Iran in violation of international law." Trump credited China with helping bring Iran to the ceasefire table.

🇷🇺
RussiaStrongly Opposed

Condemned the attacks as "another unprovoked act of armed aggression" at an emergency UN Security Council meeting. After the ceasefire, Russia's Foreign Ministry said the "aggressive, one-track" approach "suffered a crushing defeat." Russia welcomed the ceasefire but warned "there'll be no cheap oil" going forward.

🇩🇪
GermanyCritical

President Steinmeier called the campaign a "politically disastrous mistake" and a "violation of international law." Chancellor Merz controversially defended Trump's annihilation threats as having "contributed to the ceasefire." German media warned of a "strategic defeat" for the US that could leave Iran and its allies strengthened.

🇪🇺
European UnionCritical / Urging Diplomacy

EU Commission President von der Leyen called the ceasefire "much-needed de-escalation" but the EU Council statement stressed the "goal must be a swift and lasting end to the war" achieved "only through diplomatic means." EU foreign policy chief Kallas called the deal "a step back from the brink." Spain's PM Sánchez refused to applaud those who "set the world on fire." France's Macron expressed concern over the "critical" Lebanon situation.

🇮🇷
IranDefiant / Negotiating

Iran's 71-person delegation arrived in Islamabad April 11 for the highest-level US-Iran talks since 1979, led by FM Araghchi and Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf. Iran's 10-point plan demands: regional sovereignty over Hormuz, US troop withdrawal, end to all regional conflicts, war reparations, sanctions lifting, release of frozen assets, and a binding UN Security Council resolution. Preconditions for talks include a Lebanon ceasefire. Tehran leaders are publicly stating the war is "ending on Iran's terms."

🇮🇳
IndiaConcerned / Neutral

India's MEA called for a ceasefire and welcomed the April 8 truce, stressing the need for "unimpeded" trade through Hormuz. PM Modi spoke with Gulf leaders and condemned the strikes on sovereignty without naming Iran. India did not condemn Khamenei's assassination. India faces major energy security impacts from the Hormuz closure.

🇺🇳
United NationsCritical

Russia and China called an emergency UN Security Council meeting to condemn the strikes. The IAEA Board of Governors also held an emergency session. The UN welcomed the April 8 ceasefire but expressed outrage over civilian casualties, including strikes in densely populated urban areas. Three UN peacekeepers were injured on April 3.

❓ What Remains Deeply Uncertain

As of April 11, 2026, the central uncertainties are: (1) Will the Islamabad talks produce a lasting deal? Iran has set preconditions including a Lebanon ceasefire and asset release; the US demands zero enrichment and HEU surrender. The talks are described as both indirect and potentially direct — a historic first. (2) Will the Strait of Hormuz reopen? Iran paused shipping again after Israeli Lebanon strikes; only a trickle of tankers have passed since the ceasefire. (3) Will Israel halt Lebanon operations? Netanyahu explicitly excluded Lebanon from the ceasefire, contradicting Pakistan's brokered terms and threatening the entire framework. (4) What is the status of Iran's nuclear program? The HEU stockpile buried underground is now assessed as a greater proliferation risk than before the war; any lasting deal must resolve this. CSIS notes the nuclear issue will determine "not only when the war ends, but how it ends, and who can claim victory." (5) Will the Iranian political system stabilize? Mojtaba Khamenei's succession is described as "surprising" by RAND experts; whether the IRGC can maintain regime cohesion long-term remains an open question. (6) Long-term alliance realignment: US Asian allies are already buying Russian oil and negotiating energy deals with Iran and China — a potential long-term strategic cost that analysts say will "take months" to resolve regardless of ceasefire outcome.

📋 What Happened — Background

March 29, 2026

The Houthis joined the war on 28 March, conducting a ballistic missile attack towards Israel and triggering air raid sirens in Beersheba, saying they had launched ballistic missiles targeting "sensitive" military sites in Israel, and vowing to continue the attacks "until the aggression on all resistance fronts stops." More than 300 American soldiers have been wounded since the start of the war on February 28, US Central Command said. At least 1,900 people have been killed in attacks on Iran since February 28, the Iranian Red Crescent reported. US envoy Steve Witkoff said he expects meetings with Iran "this week" and is waiting for Tehran's response to a 15-point peace plan.

✅ Arguments For the War

Military degradation of Iran's capabilities: Hundreds of Iranian missile launchers have been rendered inoperable. According to some reports, 80 percent of Iran's capacity to strike Israel has been eliminated. Iran's naval assets, fast-attack craft, midget submarines and mine-laying capabilities are being liquidated.
Nuclear program setback: The current campaign has damaged further the Natanz nuclear facility. The one in Fordow remains inoperable. The defence industrial facilities that would be needed to reconstitute enrichment capacity are being systematically targeted.
Regional realignment: Qatar and Bahrain are arresting IRGC operatives. Kuwait and Saudi Arabia are intercepting Iranian drones over their own territory. The regional environment that sustained Iran's proxy architecture, including the grudging tolerance by Gulf states fearful of Iranian retaliation, is being replaced by active hostility.

❌ Arguments Against the War

Expanding conflict scope: The Houthis joined the war on 28 March, conducting ballistic missile attacks and vowing to continue attacks "until the aggression on all resistance fronts stops." At least 1,189 people have been killed in Israeli strikes in Lebanon since March 2, Lebanon's health ministry said.
Expert skepticism on outcomes: A solid 51 percent of academic experts expected the Iranian regime to survive in something like its prewar form—an outcome that is looking increasingly likely and that the scholars anticipated far better than the pundits or the Israeli and U.S. officials briefing them.
Severe economic disruption: The head of the International Energy Agency described the situation caused by the war as the "greatest global energy security challenge in history". Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz disrupted 20% of global oil supplies and significant liquefied natural gas (LNG) volumes. Analysts forecast prices could reach $100 per barrel if disruptions persisted, potentially adding 0.8% to global inflation.
Civilian casualties and infrastructure damage: U.S.–Israeli airstrikes severely damaged several residential and civilian facilities, including the Iran University of Science and Technology. Airstrikes also struck a 10,000-cubic-meter water reservoir in Haftkel, Khuzestan.
War cost escalation: The Pentagon wants $200 billion in supplemental funds to pay for its war on Iran, a defense official told The Intercept. That sum is four times the amount originally floated by Pentagon officials. War Secretary Pete Hegseth said that number could even increase. The $200 billion ask is in addition to a record-setting $1.5 trillion War Department budget request for 2027.

🌍 How the World Has Responded

NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte: Mark Rutte, NATO secretary general, said in an interview with BBC news that Europe is "supportive" of the U.S. attacks on Iran, as Iran is a "threat". He also said that he felt that the US "knows what it is doing."
German Chancellor Friedrich Merz: The war is unlikely to lead to "regime change" in Iran, said German Chancellor Friedrich Merz. "If that's the goal, I don't think you'll achieve it. It's mostly gone wrong" in past conflicts, he said, pointing to the Afghanistan war.
International Atomic Energy Agency: The International Atomic Energy Agency repeated its call for "restraint" in the Middle East war after Israel struck two Iranian nuclear facilities, including a uranium processing plant.
Pakistan diplomatic initiative: Iran has agreed to allow 20 ships under Pakistani flag to pass through the Strait of Hormuz, Pakistan's Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar said in a post on X. Under the agreement, two ships will cross the strait daily, he added. "This is a welcome and constructive gesture by Iran and deserves appreciation," Dar wrote.
UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer: On 28 February 2026, in a joint statement with the leaders of France and Germany, the Prime Minister Sir Keir Starmer condemned the Iranian counter-strikes and called for a resumption of diplomacy. The Prime Minister said he did "not believe in regime change from the skies".

❓ What Remains Deeply Uncertain

The war launched by the United States and Israel against Iran continues, even as efforts to reach a diplomatic solution increase and there are conflicting claims about possible negotiations. At the same time, the US has signalled both military escalation and openness to a deal. The conflict continues to roil global energy markets, especially because of disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz. The prospects for negotiating a formal peace ending this conflict must be judged as poor. The most obvious justification for this assessment reflects the large gap between the expressed American and Iranian positions.

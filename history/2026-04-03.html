📋 What Happened — Background

April 03, 2026

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched a massive coordinated military campaign against Iran — codenamed Operation Epic Fury by the U.S. and Operation Roaring Lion by Israel. President Trump announced the strikes via an eight-minute video on Truth Social at ~2:00 AM EST, without a formal congressional address beyond a War Powers notification and Gang of Eight briefing. The stated objectives included destroying Iran's ballistic missile infrastructure and nuclear program, eliminating IRGC leadership, annihilating Iran's naval capabilities, and neutralizing Iranian-backed regional proxies. Regime change — though not formally declared — was widely understood as an ultimate ambition.

Critically, the strikes began during active diplomacy: just hours before the attack, Oman's Foreign Minister Badr Al-Busaidi reported a diplomatic "breakthrough" in which Iran had agreed to never stockpile enriched uranium and to full IAEA verification. Talks were expected to resume March 2. After the strikes commenced, Al-Busaidi said "active and serious negotiations" had been undermined. Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi had called a historic agreement "within reach" just three days prior.

The opening wave killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, Iranian Defense Minister Aziz Nasirzadeh, IRGC commander Mohammad Pakpour, and dozens of senior officials. The IAF struck approximately 500 military targets using ~200 fighter jets — the largest combat sortie in Israeli history. Iran retaliated immediately, launching missiles and drones against Israel, U.S. military bases, and allies across the Gulf, and declared the Strait of Hormuz closed to hostile nations. As of Day 35 (April 3, 2026), the conflict has killed more than 1,937 people in Iran (per Iran's Health Ministry), with over 24,800 injured. Iran's new Supreme Leader, Mojtaba Khamenei (son of Ali), was elected on March 8. Strikes have expanded to Iranian steel plants, nuclear facilities, civilian infrastructure, and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The April 6 deadline — for Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz or face destruction of its energy plants — looms as the conflict's next major inflection point.

✅ Arguments For the War

🛡️
Elimination of Iran's Nuclear & Missile Threat

The U.S.–Israeli coalition struck nuclear sites at Natanz, the Khondab Heavy Water Complex, and yellowcake facilities at Yazd. Netanyahu asserted the strikes eliminated Iran's ability to produce nuclear weapons and ballistic missiles. Admiral Brad Cooper reported that over 8,000 Iranian military targets — including 130 vessels — have been struck. Iran's navy has been effectively destroyed, with confirmed sinking of 11+ warships.

🎯
Decapitation of IRGC & Regime Leadership

Strikes killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, the IRGC commander, Iran's Defense Minister, intelligence chief, ballistic missile chief Makram Atimi, and dozens of senior officials. The IDF confirmed seven top security leaders eliminated in the first wave alone. Analysts note Iran's remaining leadership has been described by the NYT as "paralyzed" with severely disrupted decision-making.

🌍
Regional Allies Supportive; Proxy Network Degraded

Gulf states — Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, and Bahrain — have reportedly supported continued military pressure. Canada explicitly backed U.S. action to prevent Iran from obtaining a nuclear weapon. The E3 (UK, France, Germany) resolved to back "proportionate military defensive measures" against Iranian drones and missiles. Hezbollah — already dramatically weakened by 2024 Israeli operations — has remained largely on the sidelines. Iran's proxy network has been substantially degraded.

🔬
Strategic Leverage Created Without Occupation

Analysts at the Small Wars Journal argue the campaign has constructed a "coercive architecture" — destroying missile, drone, naval, and proxy capabilities to create sustained leverage that constrains Iran's options without requiring a ground invasion. Likely outcomes assessed include negotiated limits enforced by continued pressure, or a deterrence-based ceasefire resembling post-1991 Iraq containment.

❌ Arguments Against the War

⚖️
Diplomatic Process Sabotaged — Iran Was Negotiating

Just hours before strikes began, Oman's FM reported a "breakthrough": Iran had agreed to zero uranium stockpiling and full IAEA verification. Diplomats with knowledge of the talks said U.S. envoy Steve Witkoff misrepresented the negotiations. The IAEA confirmed there was "no evidence of a structured nuclear weapons program" when the war began. Critics and legal experts have called the strikes a violation of international law and an unconstitutional preventive war launched without congressional approval.

💀
Severe Civilian Casualties & Humanitarian Crisis

Iran's Health Ministry reports over 1,937 killed and 24,800 injured since February 28. Iranian officials report damage to at least 120 museums and historical sites, 13+ health facilities (WHO-verified), and a desalination plant on Qeshm Island. A US Tomahawk missile struck near a girls' school in Minab, with Iranian state media reporting 175+ killed — mostly children. UN human rights experts characterized the strike as a potential war crime. Tehran has experienced power outages, water disruptions, and near-total internet blackout.

🛢️
Global Economic Catastrophe — Strait of Hormuz Closed

Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz — through which ~20% of global oil normally flows — triggering the most significant supply shock in oil market history (IEA). Brent crude surged to $106/barrel (+63% in March, the largest single-month gain since 1988); U.S. gasoline crossed $4.06/gallon. The World Bank raised alarm about impacts on inflation, jobs, and food security. CSIS warns the war is threatening global agriculture markets. Analysts warn prices could reach $150–$200/barrel, risking global recession. Shipping analysts assess routine Hormuz transit unlikely to resume for the remainder of 2026.

☢️
Nuclear Non-Proliferation System Destabilized

Iranian politicians are formally debating NPT withdrawal in parliament, with one official calling membership meaningless as it "has had no benefit for us." Strikes near the Bushehr Nuclear Power Plant prompted IAEA warnings about potential radiological incidents. Analysts warn a possible West Asia nuclear arms race could follow, with Saudi Arabia reportedly exploring nuclear options. The IAEA was unable to access bombed sites, leaving Iran's remaining uranium stockpile unverified.

🌐
Regional Escalation & U.S. Military Overstretch

Iran has launched strikes across nine countries (Bahrain, Iraq, Jordan, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Israel), damaging airports in Dubai, Abu Dhabi, Bahrain, and Kuwait. 13 U.S. service members have been confirmed killed, with a refueling aircraft crash in Iraq killing six. Israel launched a ground invasion of Lebanon on March 3. CSIS analysts warn that replenishing U.S. munitions inventory after this campaign will take time, creating near-term military risk. Houthi rebels resumed attacks on Israel on March 28.

🌍 How the World Has Responded

🇺🇸
United States

President Trump announced "major combat operations" aimed at destroying Iran's missiles, navy, and nuclear program, calling the operation a defensive necessity. In an April 2 address, Trump said "core strategic objectives are nearing completion" and vowed escalation over the next 2–3 weeks. The administration is amassing a Marine force that could deploy to seize territory in Iran; Trump threatened destruction of Iranian energy plants by April 6 if the Strait of Hormuz remains closed.

🇮🇱
Israel

PM Netanyahu called it a joint operation to remove "two existential threats" — Iran's nuclear and missile capabilities — thanking Trump for "historic leadership." Israel declared a nationwide state of emergency, closed schools, and launched its largest combat sortie in history. Israeli forces have conducted decapitation strikes, ground operations in southern Lebanon, and continued attacks on Iranian infrastructure including steel plants and nuclear sites through Day 35.

🇨🇦
Canada

PM Mark Carney said Canada supports the U.S. "acting to prevent Iran from obtaining a nuclear weapon" and reaffirmed that "the Islamic Republic of Iran is the principal source of instability and terror throughout the Middle East."

🇬🇧🇫🇷🇩🇪
UK / France / Germany (E3)

Issued a joint statement condemning Iranian counter-strikes and calling for diplomacy, while reiterating Iran must end its nuclear program and stop repressing its people. The E3 resolved to back "proportionate military defensive measures" against Iranian drones and missiles. UK PM Starmer said he does "not believe in regime change from the skies" and UK aircraft are in a defensive-only role. Starmer convened a 35-nation virtual summit on April 2 to restore freedom of navigation in the Strait. Germany separately criticized the U.S. for failing to consult allies before the strikes.

🇷🇺
Russia

Russia's Foreign Ministry called the strikes "a pre-planned and unprovoked act of aggression" and demanded an immediate return to diplomatic talks, accusing Washington of falsely hyping the Iranian nuclear threat to pursue regime change. Russia condemned U.S. strikes on Natanz as a "blatant violation of international law." Russia is reportedly sending Iran drones for use against the U.S. and Israel.

🇨🇳
China

Foreign Minister Wang Yi called U.S. and Israeli attacks on Iran "a violation of international law" in calls with EU, German, and Saudi counterparts. China abstained on the UN Security Council resolution condemning Iran's retaliatory strikes. Beijing noted Iran's selection of a new Supreme Leader was "based on its constitution" and described itself as a close partner of Tehran.

🇮🇷
Iran

Denied all direct negotiations, calling U.S. ceasefire proposals "maximalist and irrational." Iran rejected the U.S. 15-point peace plan and issued a 5-point counteroffer including war reparations and recognition of Iranian sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz. New Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei (elected March 8) has pledged to continue resistance. Iranian FM Araghchi stated trust is "at zero" for negotiations. Iranian parliament is debating formal NPT withdrawal.

🇴🇲
Oman

FM Al-Busaidi said he was "dismayed" — active and serious negotiations he had been mediating were "yet again undermined" — and urged the U.S. "not to get sucked in further." Iran and Oman are separately drafting a protocol to oversee maritime transit through the Strait of Hormuz.

🇶🇦🇸🇦🇦🇪
Gulf States (Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE)

Qatar condemned Iranian attacks on its territory as "a flagrant violation of its national sovereignty." Saudi Arabia reportedly lobbied Trump to attack Iran, and Gulf states have pushed Trump to continue the war until significant changes in Iranian leadership. However, Gulf states have also suffered from Iranian retaliation — airports, oil infrastructure, and industrial sites have been struck. UAE has been actively intercepting incoming Iranian missiles and drones.

🇳🇴
Norway

FM Espen Barth said the initial attack breached international law: "The attack is described by Israel as a preventive strike, but it is not in line with international law. Preventive attacks require an immediately imminent threat."

🇮🇪
Ireland

PM Michael Martin strongly urged all parties to "exercise restraint" and warned of risks of a wider regional conflict with "grave consequences for civilians and regional stability."

🇺🇳
United Nations

UN Secretary-General António Guterres condemned U.S.–Israeli strikes and warned the Middle East conflict risks spiralling into a wider war, calling for an immediate halt to strikes on both sides. The UNSC later passed a resolution condemning Iran's retaliatory strikes on Gulf states. UN human rights experts characterized the Minab school strike as a potential war crime. The World Bank is "extremely concerned" about impacts on inflation, jobs, and food security. The IAEA warned about the risk of a nuclear accident following strikes near Bushehr.

🇻🇦
Pope Leo XIV

Expressed hope for an end to the Iran war ahead of Easter, calling for reduced violence and renewed focus on diplomacy: "Hopefully he's looking for an off-ramp" — referring to Trump — and urged all world leaders to return to dialogue.

🇵🇰🇹🇷🇪🇬
Pakistan / Turkey / Egypt (Mediators)

Pakistan is actively relaying messages between the U.S. and Iran. Turkey's FM Hakan Fidan has spoken with U.S. counterparts and is "passing messages" between Tehran and Washington. Egypt's FM is coordinating with Turkish and Pakistani counterparts in "intensive efforts" to organize direct talks. Egypt called for "gradual de-escalation efforts that would ultimately lead to the end of the war."

❓ What Remains Deeply Uncertain

As of April 3 (Day 35), the conflict's trajectory hinges on several unresolved and rapidly evolving questions:

  • The April 6 Deadline: Trump has threatened to destroy Iran's energy infrastructure if the Strait of Hormuz is not reopened by April 6 at 8 PM ET. Iran has given no sign of compliance, and shipping analysts assess routine transit is unlikely to resume for the rest of 2026. Whether Trump follows through — or grants another extension — is the single most consequential near-term decision point.
  • Ground Troops: The U.S. is amassing Marines that could deploy to Iran. Trump's April 2 address did not rule this in or out. Many lawmakers, including some Republicans, have expressed deep discomfort with the prospect of U.S. forces on Iranian soil.
  • Ceasefire Reality vs. Rhetoric: Trump claims Iran's president asked for a ceasefire; Iran flatly denies any negotiations. Multiple U.S. intelligence agencies have assessed that Iran is not currently willing to engage in substantial talks. Messages are being passed via Pakistan, Egypt, and Turkey, but no direct talks have been arranged.
  • NPT Withdrawal & Proliferation: Iran's parliament is debating formal NPT withdrawal. If enacted, this could accelerate a regional nuclear arms race, with Saudi Arabia reported to be exploring nuclear options. The IAEA cannot verify the status of Iran's remaining enriched uranium stockpile.
  • Iran's Internal Power Struggle: Reports indicate a deepening split between the pragmatic Iranian presidency (signaling an exit strategy) and the IRGC (committed to "maximum resistance"). Iran's President Pezeshkian reportedly warned the IRGC that Iran's economy could collapse within 3–4 weeks without a ceasefire, while clashing with the IRGC chief over war conduct.
  • Regime Collapse vs. Constrained Survival: CSIS and RAND experts assess regime collapse remains a possibility, potentially sparking civil war, a refugee crisis, and regional chaos — but the more likely outcome is a constrained, battered regime surviving under military and economic pressure, similar to post-1991 Iraq.

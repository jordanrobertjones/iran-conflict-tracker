📋 What Happened — Background

March 20, 2026

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched surprise airstrikes on Iran (Operation Epic Fury / Operation Roaring Lion), killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and numerous senior officials. Iran responded with missile and drone strikes against Israel, US bases, and US-allied countries in the region including Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, and Bahrain.

As of Day 21 (March 20, 2026), the conflict has significantly escalated. Israel struck Iran's South Pars gas field—the world's largest—on March 19, prompting Tehran to retaliate against energy facilities across the Gulf, including Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility. Netanyahu agreed to halt further strikes on Iranian energy sites after a reported demand from Trump, though sources contradict Trump's claim that the US "knew nothing" about the South Pars attack.

Iran has rejected ceasefire negotiations, with Foreign Minister Araghchi stating Iran will show "zero restraint" if its energy facilities are attacked again. The death toll includes over 1,400 killed in Iran, at least 18 in Israel, 13 US service members, over 1,000 in Lebanon, and dozens in Gulf states. More than 18,000 Iranian civilians have been injured. Global energy prices have spiked, with Brent crude hitting $115 per barrel, and the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed to commercial traffic.

✅ Arguments For the War

✓

Degradation of Iranian military capabilities: US and Israeli airstrikes have neutralized over 70% of Iran's missile launchers and reduced missile production to zero. Iranian ballistic missile launches fell by more than 90% from 350 on Day 1 to roughly 25 by Day 15, with similar declines in drone launches.

✓

Elimination of Iranian leadership: The assassination of Supreme Leader Khamenei and subsequent targeting of senior officials, including Intelligence Minister Esmail Khatib, Ali Larijani, and Basij commander Gholamreza Soleimani, has disrupted Iran's command structure. Netanyahu stated Israel is seeing "cracks" within Iran's leadership.

✓

Degradation of nuclear threat: Iran's nuclear infrastructure has been significantly set back, addressing concerns that Iran was weeks away from having enough enriched uranium for nuclear weapons.

✓

Naval superiority: Over 50 Iranian naval vessels have been destroyed; Iran's capacity to threaten shipping has been severely diminished, though this hasn't yet reopened the Strait of Hormuz.

❌ Arguments Against the War

✗

Regional war expansion: The conflict has spread to Lebanon (1,000+ deaths), Iraq, and multiple Gulf states. Iran attacked energy facilities in Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and UAE following Israel's South Pars strike. Saudi Arabia warned it "reserved the right to take military actions" against Iran.

✗

Global economic damage: The war caused the largest oil supply shock in history. Brent crude hit $115/barrel; Qatar's Ras Laffan strike knocked out 17% of LNG export capacity (~$20bn annual losses, 9% GDP hit). Goldman Sachs warns oil could stay above $100 through 2027. Industries in India and elsewhere have shut down due to LNG disruptions.

✗

Massive civilian casualties: Over 1,400 killed and 18,000 injured in Iran, including 204 children. A strike on a girls' school in Minab killed at least 175. UNESCO sites including Golestan Palace have been damaged. UN experts condemned strikes on civilian infrastructure including water and desalination facilities.

✗

No clear endgame: Defense Secretary Hegseth refused to set a "definitive time frame" for the war's end. Experts warn regime change via airpower alone is historically unprecedented. Iran is "playing the long game" according to analysts, and the conflict shows no signs of organic military conclusion.

✗

Constitutional and legal concerns: UN experts condemned the attack as "entirely illegal under international law and constitutes an act of aggression." The war was launched without Congressional approval beyond War Powers notification. The Defense Department acknowledged no intelligence supported claims of imminent Iranian attack.

🌍 How the World Has Responded

🇺🇸 United States (Official)

Trump: Threatened to "massively blow up" South Pars if Iran attacks Qatar again, but denied prior knowledge of Israel's gas field strike and told Netanyahu to stop energy attacks. Seeking $200B war funding from Congress.

🇮🇱 Israel

Netanyahu: "Israel acted alone" in South Pars strike. Promised to comply with Trump's demand to halt attacks on energy sites. Stated Israel is seeing "cracks" in Iranian leadership.

🇬🇧 United Kingdom

PM Starmer: Permitted use of UK bases for "defensive" strikes but said he "does not believe in regime change from the skies." UK has not participated militarily in strikes.

NATO

Secretary General Rutte: Europe is "supportive" of US attacks, calling Iran a "threat." NATO has heightened missile defense vigilance.

🇮🇷 Iran

FM Araghchi: Iran will show "ZERO restraint" if infrastructure attacked again. Rejected ceasefire talks, stated US "must be held accountable." Iran claims it has only used a "fraction" of its firepower.

🇶🇦 Qatar

Condemned Israel's South Pars strike as "irresponsible." Expelled Iranian military/security attachés over repeated attacks on Qatari facilities including Ras Laffan.

🇸🇦 Saudi Arabia

Declared "little trust that remained in Iran has been completely shattered." Warned of "military actions if necessary" against Iran. Called Iranian attacks "premeditated" and an attempt to "blackmail Arab countries."

🇪🇺 European Union

Called for moratorium on strikes against energy and water facilities. Condemned Israeli actions in West Bank. Called on Israel to halt Lebanon operations due to "severe humanitarian consequences."

🇪🇸 Spain

Denied US use of bases at Rota and Morón. PM Sánchez called war a "big error" dragging Europe into instability. Trump threatened embargo in response.

🇳🇴 Norway

FM Eide: Attack "not in line with international law. Preventive attacks require an immediately imminent threat."

🇺🇳 UN Experts

Condemned attacks as "flagrant violations of international law" and "an act of aggression." Called for investigation of strikes on girls' school, oil refineries causing acid rain, and desalination plants.

❓ What Remains Deeply Uncertain

Iran's long-term strategy: Analysts note Iran is "playing the long game"—rationing missiles, attacking Gulf infrastructure to raise costs, and counting on US domestic opposition to force negotiations. Prof. Vali Nasr suggests Iran is building leverage and may force Trump to "accept an exit."

Nuclear breakout risk: Some analysts warn Iran's new leadership could use the war as justification for a nuclear weapons breakout, which would dramatically escalate the conflict.

Gulf state participation: Political scientist Mehran Kamrava notes Gulf states are in a "diplomatic bind"—wanting to respond to Iranian attacks but fearing Trump could "declare American victory" and leave them fighting alone.

War duration: 71% of Americans expect the conflict to last months or longer. Trump mentioned 4-5 weeks originally; Hegseth now refuses to give timeline. Experts warn prolonged conflict likely if regime change remains a goal.

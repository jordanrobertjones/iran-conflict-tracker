📋 What Happened — Background

March 15, 2026

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched coordinated military strikes against Iran, codenamed Operation Epic Fury (US) and Operation Roaring Lion (Israel). The stated objectives were to destroy Iran's ballistic missile infrastructure and nuclear program, eliminate IRGC leadership, annihilate Iran's naval capabilities, and induce regime change. The opening strikes killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, sparking widespread Iranian retaliation across the region.

As of Day 16 (March 15), the conflict has escalated significantly. Iran's new supreme leader, Mojtaba Khamenei, was appointed on March 8 following his father's assassination. US Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth claims Mojtaba is "wounded and likely disfigured." US forces have struck over 6,000 targets and sunk more than 65 Iranian naval vessels. On March 14, US forces struck military targets on Kharg Island, the critical hub handling 90% of Iran's crude exports, while warning that oil infrastructure could be targeted if Iran continues disrupting the Strait of Hormuz.

The conflict has spread across the region. Iran has launched missile and drone attacks against US bases and allied facilities in the UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, and Jordan. Hezbollah entered the conflict on March 2, prompting expanded Israeli operations in Lebanon that have killed over 773 people and displaced 830,000. Heavy airstrikes were reported early on March 15 in Isfahan, Shiraz, southern Tehran, and other locations. Iran's Foreign Minister Araghchi stated the war could only end with guarantees against renewed attacks and compensation for damages.

✅ Arguments For the War

Degrading Iran's Military Capabilities: The White House reports Iran's ballistic missile capability reduced by ~90% and drone capability by 95% since the war began. US forces have struck over 6,000 targets and sunk 65+ Iranian naval vessels.
Neutralizing Nuclear Threat: Strikes have targeted Iran's nuclear program, building on damage from the June 2025 "Twelve-Day War." However, CSIS warns that Iran still possesses 400 kg of 60% enriched uranium with unknown location.
Weakening Proxy Networks: RAND experts note the war continues to weaken and fragment Iran's proxy network, degrading Tehran's capacity to execute coherent operations. Hezbollah leadership was already significantly degraded before this conflict.
Exploiting Regime Weakness: Strikes came after massive anti-government protests in January 2026 and years of economic decline, when Iran's internal legitimacy was at historic lows. Selection of Mojtaba Khamenei contradicts founding principles of the Islamic Republic.

❌ Arguments Against the War

Massive Civilian Toll: At least 1,444 killed and 18,551 injured in Iran (Iranian Health Ministry figures). A girls' school in Minab was struck, reportedly killing ~180. UN human rights experts characterized some strikes as potential war crimes.
Regional Destabilization: Iran's retaliation has struck Dubai International Airport, hotels in UAE, Kuwait's airport radar, and facilities in Bahrain, Qatar, Saudi Arabia and Jordan. At least 16 Gulf state civilians killed; 773+ killed in Lebanon.
Economic Fallout: The Strait of Hormuz is effectively closed since March 8. Oil prices exceeded $119/barrel. IEA released record 400 million barrels of oil reserves. US gas prices hit 22-month high at $3.63/gallon. Analysts warn of potential global recession.
US Costs and Casualties: At least 13 US service members killed; 140+ wounded. War cost estimated at $16.5 billion in first 12 days (~$900 million/day). 250+ US organizations signed letter demanding Congress halt war funding.
Diplomatic Failure: Oman's FM announced a "breakthrough" agreement was "within reach" on February 27, with Iran agreeing to never stockpile enriched uranium. Strikes came one day later, ending diplomatic progress.
Proliferation Risks: CSIS warns that if Iran's Atomic Energy Organization collapses, nuclear scientists could pose proliferation risks to non-state actors or outside countries.

🌍 How the World Has Responded

Israel: PM Netanyahu thanked Trump for "historic leadership" and said Tehran cannot be allowed nuclear weapons that would "threaten all of humanity."
Finland: FM Valtonen said Khamenei's assassination "opens a window for long-awaited change in the Middle East."
Saudi Arabia: Crown Prince MBS lobbied Trump repeatedly for the attack. Kingdom intercepting Iranian drones but allowing US aircraft through airspace for Kharg Island strikes.
UK: PM Starmer said he does "not believe in regime change from the skies." UK not participating militarily, only providing defensive support.
France: Macron said France "cannot approve of" strikes conducted "outside of international law" while noting Iran bears primary responsibility.
Norway: FM Eide said the attack "is not in line with international law" as "preventive attacks require an immediately imminent threat."
EU: Called conflict "greatly concerning," urging de-escalation while reportedly supporting "credible transition" of power in Iran.
Arab League: Secretary-General condemned Iranian counter-strikes as "fully reprehensible" and a "grave Iranian strategic mistake."
China/Russia: While Iran has turned to both, analysts do not expect significant defense or security support. Russia convened IAEA emergency meeting.

❓ What Remains Deeply Uncertain

Duration and Endgame: Trump has given conflicting signals, calling it both a "war" and a "little excursion," saying it's "very complete, pretty much" while also stating "we haven't won enough." The US has announced preparations for operations lasting at least 100 days, potentially until September.

Ground Forces: Senator Graham downplayed the possibility but Senator Blumenthal warned Trump's approach could lead to deployment of ground forces. Trump has said he might put "boots on the ground" for regime change.

Iran's Next Move: FM Araghchi warned Tehran would strike US companies' facilities in the region if Iran's energy infrastructure is targeted, and suggested Iran may only allow tankers paying in Chinese yuan through Hormuz.

Global Economic Impact: RAND experts note "it is too soon to tell" if effects will be dire. If Hormuz stays closed for considerable time, prices will stay elevated and the world "might enter a recession."
